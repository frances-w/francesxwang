How Firm Strategies Affect Consumer Biases in Online Reviews

Published in Service Science, February 2023
Cornell University, SC Johnson College of Business
Research Question
Understanding how review collection strategies create systematic biases

How do different review-collection strategies employed by hospitality firms affect the characteristics and biases of online reviews?

Specifically, this study investigates how collection methods impact review ratings, length, polarity, and positivity across major hospitality brands and platforms.

💡 Statistical Significance

Random effects models controlling for hotel heterogeneity and temporal factors confirm that collection method significantly impacts ratings (p < 0.001). The Brand method yields approximately 0.2-0.4 points higher ratings compared to self-motivated reviews, while OTA yields roughly 0.1-0.15 points higher.

Major Research Findings
Self-motivated reviews
Lowest rated but longest: TA-SM reviews have the lowest average rating (3.86) but provide the most detailed feedback with a median length of 80 words, suggesting more critical and engaged reviewers.
Email solicitation effect
Increases ratings significantly: All email-prompted methods (Brand, OTA, TA-RE) generate higher ratings than self-motivated reviews, with differences of 0.15 to 0.42 points—statistically and practically significant.
Brand collection paradox
Highest ratings, shortest reviews: Brand website reviews have the highest mean rating (4.28) but shortest median length (22 words), potentially due to survey fatigue and pre-existing brand loyalty among direct bookers.
Expedia minimal effort
44% contain no text: Nearly half of Expedia reviews are rating-only with no written feedback, creating potential credibility concerns despite high numeric scores.
Review Express balance
Volume + detail: TA-RE reviews achieve higher ratings than TA-SM while maintaining moderate length (64 words), potentially reducing nonresponse bias.
Platform requirements matter
Character minimums work: TripAdvisor's 200-character minimum requirement results in substantially longer, more informative reviews compared to platforms with no minimum (OTA) or limited requirements (Brand).

🎯 Practical Implication

A 0.2-point rating increase (e.g., from 3.9 to 4.1) can lead to significant increases in customer traffic and revenue. For context, previous research shows a half-star improvement on Yelp can increase restaurant reservations by 30-49% during peak hours.

Strategic Recommendations for Hospitality Firms
When to use each collection method
Brand Website
For higher ratings: Use when you need to boost overall scores and have loyal direct bookers. Trade-off: shorter reviews may reduce credibility. Best for: protecting brand image, building internal community.
TripAdvisor Review Express
For volume + credibility: Leverages third-party platform trust while generating higher ratings than organic TA reviews. Best for: building TripAdvisor presence, benefiting from herd behavior.
OTA (Expedia)
For volume, not depth: Easy submission drives high volume but 44% have no text. Best for: quickly accumulating reviews, platforms where volume matters more than detail.
Self-Motivated (Organic)
For authentic detail: Can't be controlled but provides longest, most detailed feedback. Best for: understanding true guest sentiment, getting rich qualitative data.

Optimize Survey Design

For brands using GSS + review collection:

  • Segment customers: Send private survey to one group, public review invitation to another (avoid survey fatigue)
  • Set minimum word counts: Balance between requiring detail and avoiding abandonment
  • Reduce survey length: Shorter surveys before review requests increase review quality

⚠️ Trade-offs to Consider

Higher ratings vs. Review depth: Methods that generate highest ratings (Brand, OTA) produce shortest reviews with least information for future consumers

Volume vs. Quality: Making reviews easier (OTA approach) increases volume but 44% have no text

Control vs. Credibility: Hosting reviews on brand sites provides control but may reduce consumer trust

Methodology
Random effects panel models with hotel-level controls

Primary Model

Random effects linear regression controlling for:

  • Hotel-level heterogeneity (random effects)
  • Brand fixed effects
  • Chain scale fixed effects
  • Temporal factors (year and month fixed effects)

Robustness Checks: Hausman test (favored random effects), Breusch-Pagan LM test, Lagrange multiplier tests, fixed effects models, ordered probit models—all confirmed main findings.
